The network operates on algorithmic issuance rules and consensus mechanisms, while its price is determined exclusively by market supply and demand on exchanges. Historically, BTC volatility has been significantly higher than that of traditional assets, as confirmed by long-term market data from major trading aggregators. Large holders commonly referred to as whales can amplify short-term price pressure when selling substantial volumes, particularly in conditions of limited order book depth. This is not a conspiracy theory; it is standard market mechanics: large supply meeting limited liquidity results in price movement.

The economic logic here rests less on absolute capital size and more on psychology. Behavioral economics describes expectation effects: if market participants believe that a buyer of last resort exists, their inclination toward panic selling decreases. The price of any asset reflects not only order flow but also collective confidence. If confidence is high, the actual need for intervention diminishes. Put simply: if everyone believes that when whales dump, the people buy, it is possible that no buying will be necessary.
